Touch Grass App (Figma)

Overview

The Touch Grass app is a figma prototype created for my UX Design class at CSU East Bay, used to showcase our creative skills and implement design principles that we learned from UI Design.

Pitch

In the modern era, phones are everywhere, and people are confined within their homes. However, with this app, we are hopefully able to remedy that situation.

Pitch Deck

Touch Grass Pitch Deck

Product Brief

Touch Grass is a mobile app that aims to help and encourage users to go outside, either with friends or by themselves. The app achieves this goal by collecting data on locations near the user, and comparing the location to friends' interests.

What We Want To Acomplish

We want to create a mobile app that motivates the user to go outside alone or with company. By doing so, it will improve the user's mental health, encouraging them to go outside, discover new experiences, as well as give them an opportunity to hang out with friends and family.

My Role

My contribution toward this project was helping with data collection, ensuring that we were able to provide services that would better suit our target demographic; also, providing a seemless UX/UI design for our app so that the user interface would be visually appealing and easy to understand; as well as making sure the functionality of the prototype was functioning as it should.

Brainstrom

From the initial stages of this project, our group brainstromed together on what types of problems that we are noticing from the world around us. And that problem that we noticed revolved around personal wellness and mental health.

The Problem

Ever since the pandemic and shelter in place, everyone has found complacency within their homes and tends to spend days stuck inside. During this time, everyday activities were restricted to things that could only be done inside the comfort of your own home or your local backyards. From this, people indulged in their hobbies ranging from watching shows, reading books, arts and crafts, cooking, and even playing video games. However, the one thing that was similar between all these hobbies was that they were done in the comfort of your own home. It quickly became apparent that although people were enjoying their own hobbies in the comfort of their own homes, many people were finding themselves increasingly dissatisfied and noticing their mental happiness deteriorating. Although being home is a relaxing place where people can rest and sleep, people still need to be outside in order to have a healthy balance of personal wellness and mental health. Our goal is to get people to leave their house and experience something outside. Whether it's taking a hike, walking to the local mall, or even meeting up with your grandparents, any form of being outside is a healthy break from the screens we see and the games we play. To do this, we will be employing the use of screens through the use of mobile smart phones that everybody has access to all day. Even if it's a screen, it can be used as a vehicle to motivate people to leave the house, and explore their local surroundings.

Problem Statement

Steven is a young adult who has spent the majority of his free time at home watching Netflix. He wants to start spending less time watching Netflix and instead exploring his local area and getting away from his house, but needs help finding local spots and being reminded to get outside.

Reality Check Questions

  • Where can we get up todate information on events that are happening in a local area?
  • Are we influencing users or are we a new form of advertisement for users?
  • What will motivate users to get out of their comfort zone?
  • Can the users afford to experience the places we recommend?
  • What transportation do users have access to?

Interview Process

For our interview process, we wrote open-ended general questions that related to our topic and that were easy enough for the interviewee to slowly get accustomed to the interview process. Some of the open-ended general questions that we asked them were, "What is a quick rundown of your weekly routine?" and "How do you feel about local events?" Once the open-ended general questions had been asked, we then asked deeper questions to help us better understand the mindset and the rhythms of our target demographic. The deep questions that we asked our interviewees were, "Would the type of location change how you plan this experience?" and "If you didn't have company, how would that change how you go out?"

Survey

After we conducted our interviews, we then used the data we collected from them to create a survey that would help us gauge a broader portion of the life styles of our target demographic, which would help us further narrow down what the key features should be, as well as the types of functionality we would be a vocal point on to help cater to the users.

Personas

Upon collecting all the data from both our interviews and surveys, we then created two extreem personas that would act as base models of the types of users that will be using our app.

The two personas that we decided to create were Jamiroquai, a full-time student within his second semester who spends most of his time doing assignments, which leaves him with little time left in the day to do any sort of recreational activities; Emily, another full-time student who is nearing the end of their academic career and has a balanced school and social life.

Wireframe

Once the group had decided on what the site map would look like, the task for creating a wire frame came to me. Upon creating the wire frame we all took input from eachother, taking in to account the main design principles that would allow for an easy to understand interface for the suers to interact with.

Prototype

LoFi Prototype

For the LoFi prototype, I adapted the wireframes and began to add basic functionality using one of the free prototypes from the Figma marketplace. Some features that did not come within the built prototpye, were created using Figma's built-in tools.

One of the problems that I faced when remapping the free prototype to account for the aesthetic and functionality we were aiming for, which include some of its own preexisting assets along with our custom-made assets. What I noticed was that some of the pages would prevent us from scrolling further into the page. For instance, on the main page of the app, once we scrolled to the bottom of the page, we were locked in place and couldn't scroll back up. A similar problem occurred with another page, but instead of not being able to scroll back up, the scrolling function was non-existent.

HiFi Prototype

For the HiFi prototype, we were able to remedy the problems that we encountered with our LoFi prototype by expanding their page's bottom to accommodate the page's other assets. I also added color and images shared by the visual designer to better fit the theme and title of our app, "grass." Another refinement added to the HiFi prototype was the addition of a mock-up login page and sign-up page, as well as functioning previous page controls that allow the user to backtrack to the previous page they were on.

User Testing

After the creation of the HiFi prototype, we then pushed it out for user testing on both the Maze platform and in-person testing to get a better understanding of how our app would function without the guidance of the creators.

From what we gathered from the initial testing, some users were confused about some of the buttons functionality as they had little indication of what they were meant to do. From this feedback, we added some extra buttons for multiple functionality as well as small text under specific buttons to help clerify their functionality and cater to a better user experience.
